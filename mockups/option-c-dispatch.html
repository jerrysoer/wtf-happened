Wages

Wage‑Productivity Gap

Net productivity rose 59.7% since 1979 while typical worker compensation grew only 15.8%.

Productivity Compensation
! Common Claim

“Ending the gold standard in 1971 caused wages to decouple from productivity.”

✓ What the Data Shows

The gap began widening in the late 1970s due to multiple converging factors including technology, globalization, declining union power, and policy changes.

Perspectives — swipe to compare
Skeptic

Correlation ≠ Causation

The timing is suspicious but the gap didn't emerge until the late 1970s. Technology and deunionization are stronger explanatory variables than monetary policy.

Neutral

Multiple Forces at Play

The wage-productivity gap involves at least five major factors. The strongest predictors are technological change, declining unionization, and trade policy shifts.

Believer

Money Printing Enabled It

Without gold discipline, inflation eroded real wages while asset prices rose. Fiat currency didn't cause every factor, but it enabled the conditions that allowed the gap to persist.

Causal Factors
Technology & Automation 30%
Computers replaced routine tasks, boosting output while reducing bargaining power for routine jobs.
Source: Autor, Levy & Murnane (2003)
Declining Unionization 25%
Union membership fell from 27% to 10%, reducing workers' collective bargaining power.
Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics
Globalization & Trade 20%
Competition from lower-wage countries put downward pressure on domestic wages.
Source: Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2013)
Policy & Deregulation 15%
Tax policy shifts, minimum wage stagnation, and financial deregulation redirected gains toward capital owners.
Source: Congressional Budget Office
Monetary Policy Shift 10%
End of Bretton Woods enabled inflation and financialization — a contributing factor, not the sole cause.
Source: Federal Reserve History
