Common Claim

What the Data Shows

The gap began widening in the late 1970s due to multiple converging factors including technology, globalization, declining union power, and policy changes.

Multiple Forces at Play The wage-productivity gap involves at least five major factors. While the timing aligns loosely with the end of Bretton Woods, the strongest statistical predictors are technological change, declining unionization, and trade policy shifts.

Causal Factors

1

Technology & Automation 30%

Computers and software increasingly replaced routine tasks, boosting output per worker while reducing bargaining power for routine jobs.

2

Declining Unionization 25%

Union membership fell from 27% to 10%, reducing workers' collective bargaining power and share of productivity gains.

3

Globalization & Trade 20%

Competition from lower-wage countries put downward pressure on domestic wages, especially in manufacturing.

4

Policy & Deregulation 15%

Tax policy shifts, minimum wage stagnation, and financial deregulation redirected economic gains toward capital owners.

5

Monetary Policy Shift 10%

End of Bretton Woods enabled inflation and financialization, but this is a contributing factor — not the sole cause.
